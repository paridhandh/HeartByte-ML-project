About HeartByte

Understanding our technology, mission, and commitment to heart health

Our Mission

HeartByte is an advanced machine learning system designed to help individuals assess their cardiovascular disease risk. Our mission is to make heart health assessment accessible, accurate, and actionable for everyone.

We believe that early detection and awareness are crucial in preventing cardiovascular diseases, which remain the leading cause of death globally. By leveraging cutting-edge AI technology and comprehensive health data, we aim to empower individuals to take control of their heart health.

What We Analyze

Our platform uses an Random Forest machine learning model trained on over 68,000 patient records to provide accurate cardiovascular disease risk predictions. The system analyzes multiple health parameters:

Demographics

Age, gender, height, and weight to calculate BMI and assess age-related risk factors

Vital Signs

Systolic and diastolic blood pressure measurements for hypertension assessment

Lab Results

Cholesterol and glucose levels to identify metabolic risk factors

Lifestyle

Physical activity, smoking habits, and alcohol consumption patterns

Dataset & Training

Our model is built on a comprehensive cardiovascular dataset containing demographic, clinical, and lifestyle features from real patient records.

Training Pipeline

  1. Data Collection: Gathering comprehensive patient records with validated outcomes
  2. Data Preprocessing & Cleaning: Handling missing values, outliers, and data quality issues
  3. Feature Engineering: Creating derived features like BMI from height and weight
  4. Manual Standardization: Normalizing features for optimal model performance
  5. Model Training: Supervised machine learning classification using Random Forest
  6. Validation & Testing: Rigorous evaluation on unseen data
  7. Continuous Monitoring: Regular updates and performance tracking

Limitations

Model Limitations: Machine learning models are not perfect and depend heavily on the quality and representativeness of training data. Predictions may be affected by:

  • Data bias in the training set
  • Noise or errors in input measurements
  • Rare or unseen medical conditions
  • Individual variations not captured in the training data
  • Changes in medical understanding and standards
